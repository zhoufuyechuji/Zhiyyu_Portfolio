Research Aim

Investigate how intentional surveillance mechanical constraints can create psychological tension and strategic decision-making in pet simulation games.

Design Gap

Most pet simulations are comfortable, immediate, and unrestricted.

These systems support relaxation and attachment, but they rarely ask players to make decisions under uncertainty.

This project explores what happens when pet simulation includes intentional limits: limited monitoring, restricted visibility, delayed intervention, and work-based interruption.

Design Innovation

The research adds more tension, prediction, risk assessment, and strategic decision-making to pet simulation.

01

Information Access

The prototype limits when and how players can see the pet, making incomplete information part of the strategy.

02

Intervention Timing

Players cannot solve every problem instantly, so they must decide which risks need attention first.

03

Care Loop

Observation becomes a loop of searching, predicting, leaving to work, returning, and intervening at the right moment.

Theoretical Framework

The project uses Theory of Constraints to explain why limited monitoring creates strategy.

Theory of Constraints

Theory of Constraints identifies the bottleneck that shapes a whole system. Once that bottleneck is clear, the design can test how players adapt their attention, timing, and decisions around it.

TOC and This Project

System Goal Maintain pet stability while completing work tasks

Players balance pet care and productivity.

Main Bottleneck: Limited Surveillance Access Temporal restriction limits when players can monitor; spatial restriction limits what players can see

Creates uncertainty, delayed response, and risk-management decisions.

Information Throughput Pet status values, camera view, warning cues, and behavior changes

Players must decide which information matters most.

Cognitive WIP Mood, Hunger, Mischief, work duration, and off-screen pet behavior that players must mentally track

Increases cognitive pressure and planning demand.

Strategic Adaptation Pre-work checking, predictive monitoring, and returning during risky moments

Shifts players from reaction toward prediction and prioritization.

Game Overview

A pet simulation about working, watching, and stopping trouble from a distance.

This is a pet simulation game where players take the role of an office worker who leaves home for work. Because she misses and worries about the naughty cat at home, she secretly checks the home camera.

Through remote-controlled smart furniture and devices, players can stop the cat from causing trouble before destructive behavior happens. The core experience asks players to balance working, monitoring, and intervention under limited attention and limited time.

Core Constraint Systems

Limited surveillance access is the main bottleneck.

Temporal and spatial restrictions are two ways of applying the same core constraint: players cannot always access all information or respond exactly when they want.

01 Temporal Restriction

Work blocks monitoring.

Work periods temporarily remove monitoring access, turning work time into a risk-management decision.

02 Spatial Restriction

The whole room is never visible at once.

Players must move the camera, search for the pet, and manage uncertainty about off-screen events.

03 Limited Intervention

Actions do not solve everything instantly.

Feed, Toy, and Notice affect different pet values, encouraging players to choose when and how to act.

04 Integrated Constraint

Limited time, view, and response overlap.

The final prototype combines these systems to create prediction, prioritization, and cognitive pressure.

Real-World Time Mapping

Work time mirrors a daily schedule.

The prototype maps play to a 9 AM to 5 PM workday structure, using temporal progression to make work absence feel more grounded and pressurized.

Dynamic Decay System

Pet states create chain-reaction pressure.

Hunger and Mood gradually decrease while Mischief increases. Critical hunger accelerates Mood loss and Mischief growth, turning pet care into an escalating system rather than static maintenance.

Warning Behavior System

Warning states create a prediction window.

Before destructive actions occur, the pet enters a warning state. This gives players a short chance to anticipate and react strategically.

Damage Feedback & Money Penalty

Destruction has visible consequences.

Destructive pet actions trigger money-loss popups, connecting missed interventions to clear economic feedback.

End-of-Day Rating System

Performance feedback supports reflection.

A star-based evaluation screen gives players lightweight emotional feedback at the end of the day, supporting replayability without harsh punishment.

Three testing phases measured how restriction changed player behavior.

The validation tested temporal restriction, spatial restriction, and integrated constraints through gameplay data and player questionnaires.

Temporal Constraint Test

Destructive Event Rate (DER) = Destructive Pet Events During Work Total Corresponding Work Sessions
Phase 1 Key Finding

Longer work absence increased destructive pet events.

  • Work duration became a meaningful risk variable.
  • Players needed to judge pet stability before leaving monitoring.
  • DER showed a clear positive relationship with time away.
Outcome / Implication

Work time became a risk-management decision.

  • Players traded work reward for pet safety.
  • Temporal restriction encouraged planning before absence.
  • Decisions relied on prediction, not perfect information.
Phase 2 Key Finding

Restricted visibility increased uncertainty and tension.

  • Players felt more pressure when they could not see the full room.
  • Off-screen uncertainty became the strongest tension source.
  • Searching for the pet increased fear of missing important events.
Outcome / Implication

Monitoring became active searching instead of passive watching.

  • Spatial restriction changed how players gathered information.
  • Players had to decide where to look first.
  • Limited visibility encouraged attention management under uncertainty.
Phase 3 Key Finding

Combined constraints increased pressure, prediction, and strategic prioritization.

  • Players treated monitoring access as a limited resource.
  • Restricted visibility and work absence encouraged advance planning.
  • Layered constraints also increased cognitive pressure and missed intervention moments.
Outcome / Implication

Monitoring became a strategic resource-management task.

  • Players balanced risk, timing, and incomplete information.
  • Prediction became more important than immediate reaction.
  • Constraint clarity remains necessary for tension to support strategy.

Conclusion

Intentional constraints can create tension and strategy by reshaping how players access information, time interventions, and assess risk under uncertainty.

Longer work absence increased destructive pet events, making work time a risk-management decision.

Limited visibility increased uncertainty and turned monitoring into active searching.

Integrated constraints encouraged predictive monitoring, but also increased cognitive pressure.

Limitations & Future Directions

Limitations
  • The prototype was developed as a full interactive game system, so multiple mechanics operated at the same time rather than testing one isolated variable.
  • The prototype was still evolving during testing, so different participants may have experienced different levels of UI clarity, feedback, and polish.
  • Some players had difficulty understanding the relationship between pet states, work systems, surveillance restrictions, and destructive behaviors.
  • Some behavioral findings relied on self-reported questionnaire data rather than fully automated gameplay telemetry.
Future Directions

Future work can investigate how restriction systems affect player tension, strategy, and cognitive workload. Measurable gameplay data could help estimate tension levels and support designers in adjusting emotional curves, pacing, and player pressure more accurately.

Beyond game design, this direction may also contribute to studies of virtual pet care, emotional attachment, empathy, and human-pet relationships in virtual environments.
